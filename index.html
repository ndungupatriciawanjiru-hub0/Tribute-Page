Ada Lovelace

The World's First Computer Programmer · 1815 – 1852

Who Was Ada Lovelace?

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, was a brilliant English mathematician and writer. She's best remembered for her groundbreaking work on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine — an ambitious mechanical computer that was never fully built in her lifetime.

As the only legitimate daughter of the famous (and famously wild) poet Lord Byron, Ada had an unusual upbringing. Her mother, worried that she might inherit her father's passionate and unpredictable nature, pushed her hard into mathematics and logic. That decision turned out to be one of the best things that ever happened to computing.

"The Analytical Engine weaves algebraic patterns, just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves." — Ada Lovelace, 1843

Key Milestones in Her Life

  • 1815 — Born in London, daughter of poet Lord Byron.
  • 1828 — At just 13, she sketches out plans for a steam-powered flying machine.
  • 1833 — Meets Charles Babbage and gets her first look at his Difference Engine.
  • 1835 — Marries William King, who would later become the Earl of Lovelace.
  • 1843 — Publishes the first computer algorithm in her famous notes on the Analytical Engine.
  • 1843 — Introduces the idea of a "loop" — a concept still fundamental in programming today.
  • 1852 — Dies at only 36 from uterine cancer.
  • 1980 — The U.S. Department of Defense names a programming language Ada after her.
  • 2009 — Ada Lovelace Day is created to celebrate women in STEM every October.

Her Key Contributions

Ada's notes on the Analytical Engine were actually much longer than the original article she translated. In them, she laid out a detailed step-by-step method for the machine to calculate Bernoulli numbers — essentially writing what most people now consider the world's first computer program.

What really sets her apart, though, is her incredible vision. While others saw the machine as just a fancy calculator, Ada realised it could do so much more: compose music, create graphics, and handle any kind of symbolic information. She was thinking about general-purpose computing a full century before the rest of the world caught up.

"I believe myself to possess a most singular combination of qualities exactly fitted to make me pre-eminently a discoverer of the hidden realities of nature." — Ada Lovelace, in a letter to her mother
